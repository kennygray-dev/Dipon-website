1. Company Overview
DIPON Group is a Nigerian-owned diversified conglomerate delivering integrated solutions across infrastructure development, construction, renewable energy, real estate, transportation, agriculture, and industrial trading. The Group combines technical expertise, operational excellence, and long-term partnerships to execute projects that drive economic growth and sustainable development across Africa.
The Group operates through three legal subsidiaries, each with its own licensed scope of work, that together cover the full project lifecycle — from civil works and power infrastructure through to finished, occupiable property and ongoing supply chain support:
Subsidiary
Role Within the Group
DIPON Construction Limited
Civil engineering, building construction, roads and bridges, mechanical and electrical works, fabrication, and general contracting.
DIPON Global Resources Limited
General trading, import/export, product marketing, building materials supply, real estate, agriculture, transportation and logistics, and renewable energy solutions.
DIPON Infrastructure Limited
Renewable energy systems, solar infrastructure, street lighting, power infrastructure, housing and real estate development, transportation services, and equipment supply.
The website&apos;s role is not promotional. It is the Group&apos;s primary credibility and lead-generation engine for government procurement teams, corporate clients, project owners, developers, and investors — audiences who need to verify capability, capacity, and trustworthiness before they will engage. Every decision in this document, from sitemap to sentence-level copy, is built around that requirement.
This Master Document is the single, end-to-end reference for the project: it consolidates and supersedes the separate strategy, architecture, and brand notes produced earlier, and adds the two pieces that complete a build-ready brief — full draft website copy and the design/technical/conversion requirements needed to hand this to a development team.

4. Brand Positioning
4.1 Core Value Proposition &amp; Positioning Statement
The brief&apos;s value proposition — “Building Sustainable Infrastructure and Business Solutions for Africa’s Future” — is strong as an emotional, aspirational tagline, but on its own is too broad to drive a procurement or investment decision. It is retained as the tagline, paired with a sharper functional positioning statement that does the persuasive work:
“For government agencies, project owners, and corporate organizations who need a single accountable partner across the build lifecycle, DIPON Group is the Nigerian-owned conglomerate that integrates construction, power infrastructure, real estate, and supply chain capability under one group — removing the coordination risk of multi-vendor projects and backing delivery with group-level scale, technical depth, and long-term accountability.”
This reframes “diversified conglomerate” — which can read as unfocused — as integration, which reads as risk reduction. That reframe runs through every page, not just the homepage.
4.2 Brand Pillars
The brief&apos;s eight personality traits (Professional, Reliable, Innovative, Forward-thinking, Sustainable, Trusted, Corporate, Impact-driven) are accurate but too numerous to write to consistently. They are consolidated into five pillars that map directly to website content — each pillar is demonstrable on the site, not just claimed.
Pillar
What It Means On The Site
Integrated Execution
One group covering the full project lifecycle. Demonstrated via the Subsidiaries hub, cross-linked services, and projects that show multiple subsidiaries collaborating on one site.
Engineering-Grade Reliability
Technical depth, safety standards, and governance. Demonstrated via certifications, licenses, leadership credentials, and specific (not vague) project facts.
Sustainable by Design
Renewable energy is embedded in delivery, not a marketing add-on. Demonstrated via a dedicated Sustainability section and renewable-energy content woven into Construction and Infrastructure pages.
Local Roots, Global Standards
Nigerian-owned with international-grade delivery. Demonstrated via project locations, local workforce/community content, and quality/safety standards language.
Partnership Over Transactions
Long-term relationships with government and enterprise clients. Demonstrated via partner logos, repeat-client case studies, and a relationship-oriented CTA structure.
4.3 Tone of Voice
Government and corporate buyers are conditioned to discount marketing superlatives. The website&apos;s voice — and every line of copy in Section 9 — earns trust through specificity rather than enthusiasm.
Confident, not boastful — state capability plainly (“We design, build, and power infrastructure”) rather than with adjectives (“world-class,” “unrivalled”).
Specific over generic — wherever a real number, service, or location exists, use it; avoid filler claims that cannot be evidenced. Where a real figure isn&apos;t yet available, copy uses a clearly marked placeholder rather than an invented statistic.
Plain, technical when needed — correct engineering and procurement terminology is acceptable, even preferable; this audience trusts precision.
Calm urgency in CTAs — “Discuss Your Next Project” works because it is an invitation, not a hard sell; keep all CTA language in that register.
5. Sitemap

5.3 Sitemap Notes
Company History is treated as an in-page section on the About Us overview rather than a standalone sub-page — it works better as connective narrative.
Projects, Services, and Subsidiaries are cross-linked by structured relationship (not manual links), so the same project surfaces correctly from three entry points: its subsidiary page, its service page, and the Projects archive.
A Company Profile PDF is a downloadable asset, not a page — linked from the header utility bar, About, and the footer, and intentionally left ungated to maximize procurement-officer use.
Utility and legal pages use a single generic legal-text template and are out of scope for the copy in Section 9; they should be produced directly from legal counsel&apos;s text.

6.4 Cross-Page Patterns &amp; Recommended Website Features
Global header — full-width, sticky on scroll; logo left; capability-first primary navigation; “Download Company Profile” icon-link and a persistent “Discuss Your Next Project” CTA button on the right; collapses to a hamburger menu below ~1024px.
Global footer — 4–5 columns (Sitemap, Subsidiaries, Resources incl. Company Profile download, Offices, Legal + social); stacks vertically on mobile.
Lead form pattern — consistent core fields (name, organization, sector of interest, message) across every form; forms launched from a specific page carry a hidden/pre-filled context field; on submit, a confirmation state states an expected response time.
Filter / taxonomy pattern — shared component used on Projects (Sector / Subsidiary / Region), Open Roles (Department / Location), and News &amp; Insights (Category); collapses to a full-screen filter drawer on mobile.
Call-to-action band pattern — full-width, high-contrast (typically Midnight), one headline and one button, repeated near the bottom of nearly every template.
Structured data — Organization, LocalBusiness, and JobPosting schema to strengthen visibility for corporate and careers search.
Responsive behavior — single-column stacking in the same top-to-bottom order at every breakpoint; minimum 44×44px tap targets on all forms.

7.2 Layout Notes (Desktop, Top to Bottom)
Hero: full-bleed real-project image/video background; left-aligned H1 + subhead + primary CTA button.
Group at a Glance: full-width band, 4-column animated stat grid (becomes 2×2 on mobile).
Group Overview: 2-column split — narrative left (~40%), supporting image or integration diagram right (~60%).
Subsidiaries Overview and Industries We Serve sit back-to-back, offering the two parallel self-routing paths without forcing a choice between navigation philosophies.
Featured Projects: full-width carousel/grid, 3 large cards visible, optional sector filter chips (becomes a single swipeable card on mobile).
Call to Action Band: full-width, Midnight background, centered headline + single button, positioned immediately before the footer.
7.3 UX Rationale
The Hero and Group at a Glance establish credibility before any navigation decision, addressing the Government Procurement Officer&apos;s need to confirm legitimacy fast. Featured Projects is positioned mid-page because project proof is the single highest-trust section for procurement audiences. The Call to Action Band immediately before the footer functions as a conversion safety net for the full scroll.

10. SEO Strategy
10.1 Foundational Recommendations
Build Service and Subsidiary pages around real procurement search intent (e.g. “solar street lighting contractor Nigeria,” “civil engineering construction company Abuja”) rather than generic corporate copy.
Tag every Project with sector, subsidiary, and location/region taxonomies from launch — this powers SEO landing pages and the homepage filter without rework later.
Use Organization, LocalBusiness, and JobPosting structured data where applicable, to strengthen visibility for both corporate and careers searches.
Keep the Company Profile PDF ungated — gating it behind an email form would suppress exactly the procurement-officer use case named as a primary conversion goal.
Optimize image weight carefully — the brand direction leans heavily on large project photography, which must be compressed/served responsively to avoid hurting load times and search rankings.
Use clean, descriptive, sitemap-mirroring URLs (Section 5.1) so taxonomy and intent are legible in the URL itself, not just on-page.
Treat News &amp; Insights as the primary topical-authority asset — launch articles should target the same procurement and renewable-energy search terms used to brief the Service pages.

11. Design Direction
This section translates DIPON Group&apos;s official brand guideline — logo, color palette, and typography — into direction for the website. A fuller visual design system (components, page-level mockups, and interaction detail) is built out as Design Requirements in Section 12 and, beyond this document, in Claude Design.
11.1 Logo Usage
The DIPON Group mark pairs an orange-and-midnight geometric “D” — built from two interlocking forms, echoing the Group&apos;s integration positioning — with a bold wordmark. Two approved lockups exist, one for light backgrounds and one for dark:
Light-background lockup (Midnight wordmark, Orange mark) — use on white, Custard, or other light surfaces.
Dark-background lockup (Custard wordmark, Orange mark) — use on Midnight or other sufficiently dark surfaces, including photographic overlays with a scrim.
Do not recolor, stretch, rotate, or reconstruct the mark; maintain clear space around it consistent with the source guideline.

11.4 Photography &amp; Imagery
The guideline&apos;s own reference photography — real workers in PPE on an active site — confirms the “real project photography over generic stock” direction; continue sourcing genuine DIPON Group site photography rather than stock wherever possible.
Favor candid, people-at-work imagery (hard hats, hi-vis, hands-on work) over staged corporate portraiture.
Where stock is needed temporarily, select imagery genuinely set in African infrastructure/construction contexts, never generic Western stock.
11.5 Layout &amp; Brand Motifs
Diagonal section transitions: the guideline uses an angled diagonal cut between Custard and Midnight color blocks — adapt this for section transitions (hero-to-content, CTA bands) instead of relying solely on horizontal dividers.
Geometric line-art accents: a thin orange outline derived from the logo&apos;s angular form, reused sparingly as a background accent on dark sections without competing with content.
Keep Orange disciplined: reserve it for action and emphasis, not as a large fill, so it keeps its “act now” meaning sitewide.
Grid-based layouts with generous white space: Custard and white as the dominant canvas, Midnight for high-contrast sections, Orange reserved for action.

12.2 Accessibility
Target WCAG 2.1 Level AA across the site, given the procurement/government audience and the likelihood of formal accessibility review.
Body text on Custard or White must meet a minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio against Midnight; verify Orange-on-Custard combinations for text (not just icons/buttons), which may need a darker shade for compliance.
All interactive elements (buttons, links, form fields, filter chips) need visible focus states — not just hover states — for keyboard navigation.
Every meaningful image (project photography, leadership portraits, certification badges) requires descriptive alt text; decorative graphics (line-art accents) should be marked as such for screen readers.
Forms must associate labels with fields programmatically, and validation errors must be announced, not just shown as a color change.

13. Technical Requirements
13.1 Platform &amp; Content Model
A CMS (headless or traditional) capable of modeling Subsidiary, Service, Project, Role, and Article as structured content types, each carrying the taxonomies defined in Section 6.2 (sector, subsidiary, region, department, category).
Content relationships (Service, Subsidiary, and Project) must be modeled as structured references that link to one another, not manually maintained links, per the cross-linking rule in Section 6.2 — this is what lets the same project surface correctly from three different entry points without duplication.
Editors need the ability to add a new Subsidiary, Service, or Project without developer involvement, since the architecture is explicitly built to absorb growth (Section 6.1).
13.2 Hosting &amp; Performance
CDN-backed image delivery with automatic responsive sizing/format negotiation (e.g. WebP/AVIF with fallback), given how heavily the brand direction leans on large project photography (Section 11.4).
Target Core Web Vitals: LCP under 2.5s, CLS under 0.1, INP under 200ms on a typical mobile connection — directly affects both SEO (Section 10) and the credibility-on-first-viewport goal of the Hero (Section 7).
SSL/TLS by default across all pages and forms.
13.3 Integrations
Lead form submissions route to a CRM or shared inbox, tagged by the “Purpose” field (Consultation / Quotation / Partnership / Inquiry / Other) and by originating subsidiary or service — see the routing logic in Section 14.3.
Analytics and tag management (e.g. GA4 + a tag manager) configured to track the conversion goals in Section 14.1 as distinct events, not just pageviews.
Transactional email service for form confirmations and internal lead notifications, matching the response-time commitments stated in the copy (Section 9.10–9.11).
Structured data (Organization, LocalBusiness, JobPosting) implemented per Section 10.1, ideally generated from the CMS content model rather than hand-coded per page.
13.4 Security &amp; Compliance
Spam protection on all public forms (e.g. honeypot fields or a privacy-respecting CAPTCHA alternative) without adding friction for legitimate procurement leads.
Data handling for form submissions (CVs, contact details, project inquiries) should comply with Nigeria&apos;s Data Protection Act (NDPA) requirements for personal data collection and storage.
Regular backups and a documented restore process, given the site is a primary lead-generation system, not a brochure.
13.5 Browser &amp; Device Support
Support the current and prior major version of Chrome, Safari, Edge, and Firefox, on both desktop and mobile, plus default mobile browsers (Safari iOS, Chrome Android). Given the B2G/B2B audience, graceful degradation matters more than support for legacy browsers — a procurement officer on a managed government laptop should never be blocked from submitting a form.
13.6 Maintenance &amp; Admin
Role-based CMS permissions so subsidiary-level staff can manage their own Projects/content without access to global settings or other subsidiaries&apos; content.
A documented content update workflow for the items in Section 8.3 (certifications, leadership bios, case studies) so they can be refreshed post-launch without developer involvement.

14.2 Lead Form &amp; CTA Patterns
Lead form pattern: every form shares the core fields (name, organization, sector of interest, message); forms launched from a specific page carry a hidden context field (originating subsidiary or service) so leads arrive pre-tagged; every form states an expected response time and confirms it again on submission (see copy in Section 9.10–9.11).
CTA band pattern: full-width, high-contrast (Midnight), one headline and one button — never more than one primary action per band — repeated near the bottom of nearly every template so visitors learn to recognize “this is where I act.”
Header utility CTA (“Discuss Your Next Project”) and “Download Company Profile” link persist across every page, since procurement officers may convert on first visit without browsing further (Section 5.1).
